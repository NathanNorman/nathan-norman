The problem with Denise’s method

Ridgeline’s carriers invoice between the 8th and 12th of the following month. Close falls on business day 8. The accrual has to be booked before the invoices exist. Denise handled this by averaging the prior three months of actual invoices per carrier and booking that as the estimate. It’s intuitive, but it has three fatal flaws.

It doesn’t know what happened this month. A surge of heavy Zone 4 Heartland shipments or a spike in residential Coastal orders won’t show up in a trailing average. It can’t see rate changes: when Heartland raised contract rates in January 2026, Denise’s model had no awareness for three months until the new rates were averaged in. And it treats quarterly tier resets as noise. When April opens at Tier 1 pricing (0% discount), the trailing average from February and March, when Heartland was deep into Tier 3, is systematically low.

The January miss: Heartland’s Q1 2026 contract renewal raised zone rates by ~22.7%. Denise accrued $31,067 against $38,200 actual, a $7,133 gap. The April Q2 reset is the same problem: the trailing average carries discounted rates that no longer exist.

What the engine does differently

The engine estimates charges bottom-up, shipment by shipment, applying the effective rate for each carrier against actual April activity. The published rate cards are structural templates, not billing schedules. Every carrier applies undocumented adjustments on top of them, and the gap is large enough to matter. We detected the pattern by back-calculating implied rates from six months of historical invoices and derived calibration multipliers for each carrier.

Step 1: normalize the data

The 3PL shipment log uses eight different strings for three carriers. Before any calculation can run, every row gets resolved to a canonical name.

Step 2: fill mileage gaps from invoice history

Peak’s rate card lists mileage for 11 destinations. April had shipments to 6 others. Rather than guessing, we back-calculated implied miles from six months of prior invoices: base_charge ÷ rate_per_mile_for_weight_tier. The implied mileage is internally consistent within each month across all weight tiers, which confirms the formula is clean. Fort Collins comes out at ~50 miles, Grand Junction at ~222, Casper at ~242. Reno, NV had no invoice history at all and is flagged as unestimated.

Step 3: calibrate zone rates and track the tier reset

Heartland’s published rates ($320/$485/$610/$780 by zone) are ~22.7% below what they actually billed in Q1 2026. The rate card was never updated after the contract renewal. We use invoice-derived rates instead, then apply the prospective volume discount tier: April 1 resets the QTD counter, so shipments 1–50 pay full rate and shipments 51–65 get 5% off.

def calc_heartland(shipment, qtd_sequence):
    zone = zip_to_zone(shipment['destination_zip'])
    discount = 0.05 if qtd_sequence > 50 else 0.0
    # Calibrated Q1/Q2 2026 rates, not the published card
    base = CALIBRATED_RATES[zone] * (1 - discount)
    acc  = ACCESSORIAL_FEES.get(shipment['special_handling'], 0)
    return base + acc  # fuel included in flat rate

Repeatability

Swap in next month’s shipment CSV. The engine reruns in under a second. The calibration multipliers and mileage table persist, updated quarterly against recent invoices. If a carrier raises rates, the variance report on the next invoice cycle will show the drift. Recalibrate and the model self-corrects within one month rather than three.

The Peak mystery factor

Peak’s rate card says billing is miles × rate_per_mile. It isn’t. Back-calculating from six months of invoices reveals a monthly scalar applied uniformly to every route and every weight tier before invoices are generated. The scalar ranged from 0.61 to 1.07 over the observation window — a 75% swing on the same route in adjacent months. Our original engine used the trailing average of that scalar as the default. We kept digging.

After testing 25 hypotheses — diesel prices (all lags and regions), natural gas, PC*Miler version updates, shipment volume, route mileage, freight class, pool utilization, ATA tonnage, and more — one formula fits the data with r = 0.976, adj. R² = 0.94, statistically significant at p < 0.05:

multiplier = 0.4192 + 1.7914 * (acc_rate * otri_on_billing_date)

# acc_rate          = Peak accessorial fees / Peak base freight (current month)
# otri_on_billing_date = FreightWaves Outbound Tender Rejection Index
#                        on the date Peak runs their billing batch (8th-11th
#                        of the following month)

The mechanism: Peak is a yield-managed carrier. When the freight market is tight (carriers rejecting loads) AND Ridgeline’s shipments that month were complex (more residential deliveries, liftgates, appointments), Peak charges a premium. When the market is loose and Ridgeline shipped simple wholesale pallets, Peak discounts to fill trucks. The “multiplier” is demand pricing.

The April 9 billing date (March service, multiplier 0.679) is the out-of-sample test. Liberation Day tariffs landed April 2. By April 9, freight bookings had frozen, OTRI was collapsing, and the model correctly predicts a low multiplier — even though March itself was the tightest freight month in the dataset. The billing date is what matters, not the service month.

This is actionable. Ridgeline knows their own acc_rate a week before Peak invoices — the shipment data is already in-house. OTRI is published weekly by FreightWaves at no cost. Check both the week before the expected invoice date, plug into the formula, and the April accrual estimate tightens from a ±40% confidence band to ±5%.
